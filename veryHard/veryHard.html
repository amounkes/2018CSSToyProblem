Born in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1564, Shakespeare's birthday is commonly observed on April 23rd, given that his name appears in the baptismal registry three days later on the 26th. April 23rd, coincidentally, is also St. George's day (the patron saint of England), as well as the date of death for Willliam Shakespeare 52 years later in 1616.

One of the first mentions we have of Shakespeare on the London theatre scene is by contemporary Robert Greene, who refers to young William as an "upstart Crow." Shakespeare's plays quickly rose in popularity, and his company, Lord Chamberlain's Men, was soon the leading acting company in London.

Collaboration was a major component of playwriting in early modern England. One of the more notable though lesser known collaborative efforts was on Macbeth, where Shakespeare worked with Thomas Middleton--all scenes with the three witches (or "the weird sisters") were actually written by Middleton.

Conspiracy theories have abounded for years regarding the true authorship of Shakespeare's works, from Christopher Marlowe and Francis Bacon to Edward de Vere, the Earl of Oxford. Speculation sometimes focuses on the fact that Shakespeare didn't attend university like many of his contemporaries did.
